## What This Document Is

Google employs ~16,000 human Search Quality Raters worldwide who evaluate search results.
Their ratings don't directly change individual rankings, but are used in aggregate to measure
and calibrate Google's ranking algorithms. Understanding what raters look for tells you exactly
what Google is trying to surface — and what kind of content will perform well.

The guidelines define two core tasks:
1. **Page Quality (PQ)** — How well does the page achieve its purpose?
2. **Needs Met (NM)** — How well does this result satisfy the user's search intent?

## PART 1: PAGE QUALITY

---

### The Core Framework: E-E-A-T

E-E-A-T is the most important concept in the entire guidelines document.
It stands for **Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, and Trust**.

**Trust is the most important of the four.** The other three (Experience, Expertise,
Authoritativeness) all feed into and support Trust.

#### Experience
Does the content creator have first-hand, real-world experience with the topic?
- A borrower who writes about getting a short-term loan speaks from experience
- A company explaining its own products and process = credible experience
- Generic copy written with no personal insight = low experience

#### Expertise
Does the creator have the knowledge and skills needed for the topic?
- For financial topics: licensing, years in operation, regulatory compliance
- For Captain Cash: 10+ years in Canadian lending, licensed in 9 provinces
- Staff expertise counts — customer service agents, loan specialists

#### Authoritativeness
Is the website/creator a recognized go-to source for this topic?
- Industry memberships (CLA — Canadian Lenders Association)
- References from third-party sources (loanscanada.ca, smarter.loans)
- Citations in news or comparison articles

#### Trust (Most Important)
Is the page accurate, honest, safe, and reliable?
- Transparent pricing (show real costs, not just teaser rates)
- Clear contact information, physical address, phone number
- Accurate eligibility criteria (don't promise what you can't deliver)
- Security: SSL, IBV technology, encryption disclosures
- No deceptive design (no hidden fees buried in fine print)

**Key rule:** On YMYL topics (which includes ALL of Captain Cash's pages), Trust is paramount.
Low-Trust YMYL pages are rated Lowest, regardless of other quality signals.

---

### YMYL — Your Money or Your Life

**Captain Cash is a clear YMYL website.** Every page falls under YMYL Financial Security.

Google defines YMYL Financial Security as: "Topics that could damage a person's ability to
support themselves and their families."

This means:
- Google holds Captain Cash pages to the **highest possible quality standards**
- Minor inaccuracies (wrong APR, incorrect eligibility, misleading claims) could cause real harm
- Google raters will scrutinize our pages much more carefully than non-financial sites
- Low E-E-A-T on a YMYL page = Lowest rating = essentially invisible in search

**What this means for every page we build:**
- Every factual claim must be accurate (loan amounts, APR, fees, provinces, eligibility)
- Contact information must be prominent and real
- Physical address and phone number must be present
- Company background must be clear (founded when, where, by whom, licensed where)
- Content cannot be misleading, even by omission

### Page Quality Rating Scale

Google raters assign pages one of 5 ratings:

| Rating | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Highest** | Serves a beneficial purpose and achieves it very well. Very high E-E-A-T, very positive reputation, very high quality MC. |
| **High** | Serves a beneficial purpose and achieves it well. High E-E-A-T, positive reputation, high quality MC. |
| **Medium** | Achieves its purpose but not exceptionally. No strong positive or negative signals. |
| **Low** | Intended to be helpful but fails in an important dimension. Lacking E-E-A-T, low quality MC, or mildly negative reputation. |
| **Lowest** | Untrustworthy, deceptive, harmful, or spammy. Immediately fails. |

**Target:** Every Captain Cash page should be ratable as High or Highest.

---

### What Makes a Page Lowest Quality (Avoid These)

These will kill rankings. Never do any of the following:

1. **Deceptive purpose** — The page appears to be one thing but is actually designed to mislead
2. **Harmful content** — Could cause financial harm to visitors
3. **Inadequate contact info for a financial website** — Missing address, phone, company info
4. **Lowest E-E-A-T** — No demonstrated expertise on a financial topic
5. **Deceptive design** — Hidden fees, misleading rate claims, fake urgency
6. **Scaled content abuse** — Mass-produced geo pages with zero unique value
7. **Copied content** — Content taken from other lenders without added value
8. **No added value MC** — Pages that exist only to rank, not to help users
9. **Site Reputation Abuse** — Publishing low-quality content to piggyback on domain authority
10. **Filler content** — Padding that artificially inflates word count without adding value

**Specific warning for geo pages:** Google explicitly flags "scaled content abuse" — creating
hundreds of near-identical city/province pages with no real unique content. Each geo page needs
genuine unique value (local rates, local examples, specific provincial regulations) to avoid this.

---

### What Makes a Page Low Quality (Also Avoid)

1. Low effort MC — thin content that doesn't truly help the reader
2. Filler — sentences that sound like content but add no information
3. Low originality — same copy as every other lender
4. Mildly misleading claims about the company or product
5. Missing or hard-to-find contact information
6. Distracting ads that dominate the page
7. Exaggerated reputation claims ("Canada's #1 lender" without evidence)

---

### What Makes a Page High Quality (Target This)

1. **High quality MC** — Genuinely useful, accurate, original content that fully achieves the page's purpose
2. **Positive reputation** — External evidence (reviews, citations, industry membership)
3. **High E-E-A-T** — Demonstrated experience, expertise, authoritativeness, and trust
4. **Clear website identity** — Who owns it, who created the content, how to contact them
5. **Appropriate amount of information** — Not too much, not too little for the purpose
6. **Functional, well-designed page** — Works correctly, loads properly, no broken elements
7. **Honest, transparent content** — Especially for financial information

---

### What Makes a Page Highest Quality (Aspire to This)

Everything from High, plus:
1. **Very high quality MC** — Among the best available for this topic
2. **Very positive reputation** — Multiple independent sources confirm quality
3. **Very high E-E-A-T** — Recognized authority on the topic
4. **A satisfying, helpful amount of high quality MC** — Complete, not just adequate

For a financial services page, Highest typically requires:
- Real credentials (licensed lender, CLA membership, provincial registration)
- Accurate, complete cost disclosure
- Detailed, useful information about eligibility, process, and repayment
- Multiple strong independent reviews and citations

### Understanding Page Content

Google divides every page into three zones. All three matter:

**Main Content (MC)** — Directly serves the page's purpose
- The loan calculator is MC on our homepage
- The eligibility criteria are MC on the apply page
- The FAQ answers are MC on the support page
- Page title is considered MC
- Quality of MC is the #1 factor in PQ rating

**Supplementary Content (SC)** — Supports the user experience
- Navigation links
- Related page links
- "How it works" sidebar on an apply page
- Should enhance, not distract

**Advertisements/Monetization (Ads)** — Monetization
- Presence of ads is NOT a negative signal by itself
- Ads that dominate or obscure MC are a negative signal
- We have no ads on captaincash.ca — this is a trust positive for financial pages

## PART 2: UNDERSTANDING USER INTENT

### Query Types

Google categorizes all searches into four types. Knowing this shapes how each page is built:

**Know queries** — The user wants information
- "how do installment loans work"
- "what is a no credit check loan"
- "how long does an e-transfer take"
→ Pages must provide genuinely useful, accurate information. Not just definitions — real answers.

**Know Simple queries** — The user wants a specific fact
- "captain cash phone number"
- "captain cash provinces"
- "how much can I borrow from captain cash"
→ Answer should be immediate, clear, and directly in the content.

**Do queries** — The user wants to complete a task
- "apply for a loan online"
- "get a loan with bad credit"
- "borrow $500 today"
→ The page must enable the action — clear CTA, minimal friction, obvious next step.

**Website queries** — The user is looking for a specific site
- "captain cash"
- "captaincash.ca login"
→ Brand pages must match expectations set by brand reputation.

Understanding intent shapes what each page must accomplish:

### Needs Met Rating

For a result to "Fully Meet" a query, it must immediately and completely satisfy the user —
they should not need to visit other results.

For "Highly Meets": very helpful for the dominant user intent, even if not completely self-contained.

**For our Do queries (apply for a loan):** The page Highly Meets if:
- User can immediately understand the product
- Eligibility criteria are clear
- CTA is obvious
- They feel confident enough to start

**For our Know queries (how installment loans work):** The page Highly Meets if:
- Provides accurate, complete explanation
- Uses plain language (not financial jargon)
- Addresses follow-up questions proactively
- Is clearly from an authoritative source on this topic

### Special Rules for Geo Pages (Province/City)

Geo pages are high-risk for "Scaled Content Abuse" — Google's term for auto-generated
location pages with no added value. To pass SQRG review:

Each geo page MUST have genuinely unique content:
1. Province-specific regulations and borrowing rules (e.g., BC's $14/$100 vs. our installment model)
2. Province-specific eligibility notes if any
3. At least one province-specific reference (not just "people in [city] use loans for...")
4. Actual content differences between provinces, not just name substitution

What to AVOID on geo pages:
- Pure name substitution ("Get a loan in [City]! [City] residents trust us!")
- Fabricated local statistics with no source
- Tourist attraction name-drops with no relevance (what competitors do and shouldn't)
- Identical content structure repeated across 50+ pages with only the city name changed

---

### Filler Content Warning (Jan 2025 Update)

Google's January 2025 update added specific guidance against filler:

"Filler can artificially inflate content, creating a page that appears rich but lacks content
website visitors find valuable."

Examples of filler to avoid:
- "Are you looking for a fast online loan? If so, you've come to the right place!"
- "In today's fast-paced world, financial emergencies can happen to anyone."
- "At Captain Cash, we understand that life can be unpredictable."
- Any sentence that could be deleted without losing any actual information
- Restating the same fact in multiple ways to hit a word count

Replace filler with: specific facts, real numbers, actual answers to real questions.

---

### AI Content Warning

The September 2025 update added explicit AI content guidance:

"Generative AI can be a helpful tool for content creation, but like any tool, it can also be misused."

Google is specifically training raters to identify:
- Low-quality AI-generated content that adds no value
- Mass-produced content at scale without human oversight
- Content that "appears rich" but lacks real substance
- Filler that AI models commonly produce

This applies directly to our project: All AI-generated content (including content I write)
must be reviewed for accuracy, enriched with real Captain Cash-specific information,
and should not read like generic financial boilerplate.

Every page should reflect actual Captain Cash facts: real loan amounts, real fees, real provinces,
real years in operation, real customer scenarios — not generic placeholder content.
