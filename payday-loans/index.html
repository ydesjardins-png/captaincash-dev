What is a payday loan in Canada?

A payday loan is a short-term loan typically ranging from $100 to $1,500, repaid as a single lump sum on your next payday — usually within 14 to 31 days. They are heavily regulated across Canadian provinces, with most capping fees at $14 to $15 per $100 borrowed.

Payday loans are provincially regulated in Canada. In Ontario, lenders can charge a maximum of $14 per $100 borrowed. In British Columbia, the cap is $15 per $100. These rates are significantly higher than traditional lending products — a $500 payday loan repaid over 14 days carries an effective annual rate of over 300%.

The speed and accessibility of payday loans are genuine advantages. But the lump-sum repayment structure creates a serious risk for many borrowers: handing over an entire paycheque to repay a loan often leaves nothing left for rent, groceries, or utilities — leading to another loan the following week.

The cycle risk

Why payday loans can become a trap

The payday lending cycle is well-documented. It is not because borrowers are irresponsible — it is because the product structure creates a structural problem.

Here is how the cycle typically unfolds:

1
Emergency expense hits Car repair, medical bill, or utility cut-off. You need $500 today.
2
Payday loan approved — fast $500 in your account within hours. Relief. But repayment is due in 14 days.
3
Payday arrives — $570 withdrawn The full repayment is taken automatically. Your paycheque is now $570 short.
4
Regular bills still need paying Rent, groceries, phone. The $570 shortfall means you may need another loan.
5
